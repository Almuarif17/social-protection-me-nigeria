# Brief 1 — NASSP-HUP Independent M&E Review

**An independent Monitoring & Evaluation review of Nigeria's National Social Safety Net Programme — Household Uplifting Programme (NASSP-HUP), the country's flagship federal Conditional Cash Transfer programme.**

Author: Usman Almuarif Mashood · Published June 2026

---

## What this brief is

NASSP-HUP is the largest Conditional Cash Transfer programme in Nigerian history, launched in 2016 under the National Social Investment Programmes Agency and co-financed by the World Bank under Project P151488. The programme is administered by the National Cash Transfer Office and operates through the National Social Register, providing a fixed ₦5,000 monthly transfer to households identified through a four-step Community-Based Targeting process.

In January 2024, all four NSIP programmes including HUP were suspended by a Special Presidential Panel for a six-week investigation. The investigation produced a ₦30 billion recovery action documented in a December 2025 House of Representatives motion. As of the time of this brief, no public report from the Panel has been released.

This brief examines the programme through five lenses:

1. **What it committed to do** — the Project Development Objective and the official Results Framework indicators from the 2016 Project Appraisal Document.
2. **What the evidence shows** — performance against committed targets through the latest published Implementation Status Reports, independent academic evaluations including Eluwa, Ibok, & Bassey (2025), and BudgIT's COVID-era civic accountability monitoring.
3. **Where the M&E framework falls short** — seven specific gaps identified in the indicator architecture, from absent inflation-adjustment mechanisms to the four-year evidence vacuum since the last World Bank monitoring round.
4. **What the programme has nevertheless achieved** — including documented household savings improvements, women's autonomy gains, and grievance-redress responsiveness.
5. **What should be added to the Results Framework** — five specific, operational indicator recommendations grounded in international M&E benchmarks.

## Methodology

This brief is a desk review of public sources. It does not include primary-data analysis. The evidence base is:

- World Bank Project Appraisal Document (P151488, 2016) and Implementation Status Report Sequence 6 (June 2019)
- Federal Government of Nigeria draft National Social Protection Policy
- Eluwa, F., Ibok, N., & Bassey, E. (2025), peer-reviewed evaluation of unconditional cash transfers in Nigeria
- BudgIT (2020) COVID-19 fiscal support and palliative analysis
- The Cable (2024, 2025) reporting on the World Bank evaluation and the ₦30 billion NSIPA recovery investigation
- Office of the President (January 2024) announcement of the Special Presidential Panel

All sources are cited in full in the brief's References section.

---

## Limitations of this brief

This is a desk review and inherits all the limitations of that method. It cannot generate new empirical evidence about beneficiary outcomes; it can only synthesise and cross-reference what has already been documented in the public record. Where independent quantitative evidence is thin — particularly on outcome indicators since 2019 — the brief flags those gaps explicitly rather than over-claiming.

Brief 2 of this series, which analyses the World Bank's anonymised AGILE Baseline Survey microdata directly, demonstrates the kind of primary-data analysis that brings the analytical method closer to the full M&E standard.
